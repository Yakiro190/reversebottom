# Example: Flawed Argument Breakdown (Reverse Mode)

**Command:**
```bash
reversebottom --mode reverse "Artificial intelligence will eliminate most jobs within the next 10 years. We've already seen it happen in manufacturing. White-collar work is next. There's nothing we can do — technology always wins."
```

---

**Output:**

```
CORE ASSUMPTION IDENTIFIED
──────────────────────────
That technological capability is the binding constraint on job displacement —
and therefore that when AI becomes capable of performing a task, the job
performing that task will disappear. This assumption treats adoption as
automatic and immediate once capability exists.

INVERSION
─────────
What if capability is irrelevant, and adoption is the only variable that
matters — and adoption is governed not by what technology can do, but by
institutional, economic, and political forces that move far slower than
technical progress?

THE MIRROR WORLD
────────────────
In the inverted model, the question "can AI do this job?" is the wrong
question entirely. The right question is: "Under what conditions will
organizations, regulators, workers, and customers accept AI doing this job?"

Consider the manufacturing analogy the argument itself uses. Industrial
robots became technically capable of assembling cars in the 1970s. Mass
displacement of automotive assembly workers took 40 years — not because
the technology wasn't ready, but because of union contracts, capital
investment cycles, regulatory environments, quality assurance requirements,
and the extraordinary organizational complexity of retraining and restructuring
at scale. "Technology always wins" — but it wins slowly, unevenly, and often
through augmentation rather than replacement.

The mirror world extends this logic to white-collar work: AI is already
capable of writing first-draft legal contracts, generating financial reports,
and triaging medical images. None of these capabilities have produced the
job eliminations their capabilities imply. Why?

Because the jobs are not just task bundles. They are accountability structures.
A lawyer is not valuable because they write contracts — they are valuable
because their license creates legal liability for the advice. An AI cannot
be disbarred. Until the accountability question is resolved by institutions
(bar associations, courts, regulators, insurance underwriters), capability
does not translate to deployment.

In the mirror world: the bottleneck is not AI capability, it is the
redesign of institutional trust, liability frameworks, and organizational
structures required to use that capability at scale. This is not a 10-year
problem. It is a 40-year problem, unevenly distributed by sector, country,
and firm size.

The mirror world also contains a second inversion: technology does not
"win" — it shifts the composition of work. The number of accountants in
the US increased after spreadsheet software was introduced. The number of
photographers increased after digital cameras made photography free. The
technology changed what the job was, not whether the job existed.

STRESS TEST OF THE INVERSION
─────────────────────────────
The inversion holds most strongly for: licensed professions, high-liability
domains, roles requiring institutional accountability, and sectors with
strong regulatory frameworks. It holds less strongly for: task-only roles
with no accountability component, low-regulation industries, and emerging
markets without established institutional structures that could slow
adoption. The 10-year claim fails hardest in the first category. It may
hold in narrow pockets of the second.

WHAT THE INVERSION REVEALS
───────────────────────────
The original argument conflates "capability" with "deployment," and
"displacement" with "elimination." It is not an analysis of job markets —
it is a claim about technological determinism dressed as an empirical
observation. The manufacturing example actually undermines the argument:
it shows that displacement, when it happens, takes decades and is heavily
mediated by non-technical factors.

"There's nothing we can do — technology always wins" is not a conclusion
that follows from the evidence. It is a rhetorical move that forecloses
the more interesting question: what institutional design choices govern
how and when capability becomes deployment?
```
