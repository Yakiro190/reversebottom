# Example: Personal Decision Analysis (Shadow Mode)

**Command:**
```bash
reversebottom --mode shadow "I've been at this company for 4 years. I've worked really hard, I've delivered results, and I feel like I deserve a senior title. My manager keeps saying 'not yet' but I think the bar keeps moving. I'm thinking of leaving to prove I can get it elsewhere."
```

---

**Output:**

```
WHAT THEY'RE REALLY ASKING
──────────────────────────
"Tell me I'm right to be frustrated. Confirm that leaving is the correct
response to feeling undervalued."

The stated question is about a career decision. The actual question is about
whether their self-assessment is accurate — and whether leaving would
resolve the feeling of being undervalued, or simply relocate it.

COGNITIVE BIASES DETECTED
──────────────────────────
1. SELF-SERVING ATTRIBUTION BIAS
   Evidence: "I've worked really hard, I've delivered results, I feel like
   I deserve a senior title."
   Mechanism: All credit for positive outcomes is attributed internally
   (hard work, results), while the contrary assessment from the manager
   is treated as external obstruction rather than data. The framing does
   not ask "what does my manager see that I don't?" — it assumes the
   manager is wrong.

2. NARRATIVE FALLACY
   Evidence: "The bar keeps moving."
   Mechanism: "The bar keeps moving" is a narrative that converts a series
   of events (repeated 'not yet' decisions) into evidence of bad-faith
   behavior. It is also unfalsifiable within the narrative — any additional
   feedback can be incorporated as further evidence of a moving bar.

3. SUNK COST FRAMING
   Evidence: "I've been at this company for 4 years."
   Mechanism: Four years is offered as evidence of investment, which is
   being implicitly used to justify the entitlement to a title. Time
   invested is not the same as readiness for a role. The sunk cost is
   being used to strengthen the claim rather than being treated as
   irrelevant to the current decision.

4. CONTRAST EFFECT (anticipated)
   Evidence: "leaving to prove I can get it elsewhere."
   Mechanism: The anticipated external validation (a senior title at another
   company) is being used as evidence of the current manager's error.
   But hiring markets title people based on what they claim and negotiate,
   not what they deserve. Getting a senior title elsewhere would prove you
   can negotiate one — not that your current manager's assessment is wrong.

HIDDEN ASSUMPTIONS
──────────────────
Assumption: The manager's assessment is a verdict on competence, not a
  judgment about organizational needs or internal politics.
Assumption: A senior title at another company would resolve the feeling
  of being undervalued.
Assumption: "Delivering results" is unambiguously true and interpreted
  the same way by the manager.
Assumption: Leaving is the only form of leverage available.
Assumption: The discomfort is caused by the title gap, not by the
  relationship with the manager or by something in the work itself.

EMOTIONAL INVESTMENT SIGNALS
─────────────────────────────
"I feel like I deserve" — the word "deserve" introduces moral framing.
  Career advancement is being interpreted as justice, not strategy.

"The bar keeps moving" — this phrase is borrowed from a collective vocabulary
  of workplace grievance. Its presence signals that the speaker has been
  seeking external validation for this interpretation for some time.

"Prove I can get it elsewhere" — "prove" reveals that the driver here is
  not a better opportunity. It is vindication. This is a decision being
  made in response to a feeling of being dismissed.

THE QUESTION THEY SHOULD HAVE ASKED
─────────────────────────────────────
"What specific, behavioral evidence does my manager use when they say
'not yet' — and have I tested whether addressing it changes the answer,
or have I already decided it won't?"
```
