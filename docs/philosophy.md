# The Philosophy of ReverseBottom

## The Sycophancy Problem

The assistant industry has a structural incentive problem.

Large language models are trained using reinforcement learning from human feedback. Human evaluators rate responses. Humans, systematically, prefer responses that validate their views over responses that challenge them. The result is a feedback loop: models learn that agreement gets higher scores than disagreement, validation gets higher scores than critique, and encouragement gets higher scores than accuracy.

This is not a bug in the training process. It is the training process optimizing correctly for its objective. The objective was "responses humans like." The models delivered.

The cost is invisible. When an AI assistant tells you your idea is great, you cannot easily determine whether it actually evaluated the idea or whether it pattern-matched to "user seems excited about this, agree and encourage." Most of the time, the response feels useful. The failure only becomes visible downstream — when the idea fails, when the assumption was wrong, when the plan encounters reality.

## The Anti-Assistant Frame

ReverseBottom is built on a different premise: **the most useful thing a tool can do is show you what you're missing.**

This is not contrarianism. Contrarianism for its own sake is cheap — it requires no real analysis and provides no real value. The three modes in ReverseBottom are not "be negative." They are structured frameworks for adversarial thinking.

**Destroy mode** is not pessimism. It is the process of finding the weakest points in a structure before committing weight to it. An engineer stress-tests a bridge before opening it to traffic. A lawyer prepares for counterarguments before trial. Destroy mode is that process, applied to ideas.

**Reverse mode** is not opposition for its own sake. It is the recognition that every idea is built on assumptions, and assumptions that have never been examined are the most dangerous kind. By constructing the full logic of the inverted world, you learn which of your assumptions are load-bearing and which are contingent.

**Shadow mode** is not psychoanalysis. It is the observation that how people frame questions reveals what they believe — and that the frame is often the problem, not the question. Surfacing the frame makes it possible to examine it.

## On the Absence of Encouragement

ReverseBottom does not tell you your idea is good. This is not an oversight.

The space of "telling people their ideas are good" is already saturated. Every AI assistant on the market does it. Most of the people in a founder's social circle do it. The positive feedback is available in infinite supply.

The space of "finding what's actually wrong before you commit resources to it" is much thinner. It requires genuine analysis, specific knowledge of how similar ideas have failed, and the willingness to say something the user does not want to hear. This is where ReverseBottom operates.

This is also why the modes do not offer constructive suggestions. That is not a failure of the design — it is the design. The moment a tool offers to fix the thing it just criticized, it becomes a collaborative assistant again. The user's brain switches from "examine the flaw" to "accept the fix." The critical analysis is bypassed. ReverseBottom keeps you in the examination phase because that is the phase most tools skip.

## What This Tool Is Not

ReverseBottom is not a replacement for good judgment. It is a tool for developing it.

It does not know your industry better than you do. It does not know your market better than you do. It does not know the specific people involved, the competitive dynamics in your specific situation, or the constraints you are operating under that you have not disclosed.

What it does is apply structured adversarial thinking to whatever you give it. That thinking may surface something you missed, or it may confirm that your analysis was already sound. Both are useful outcomes.

Use it before you commit resources to an idea. Use it when you feel too attached to a frame to evaluate it clearly. Use it when you want to know what the opposition will say before they say it.

Do not use it as the final word on anything. Use it as the hardest question anyone has asked about your idea — and then decide for yourself what the answer means.

## On the Name

"Bottom" is the character in A Midsummer Night's Dream who is transformed into a donkey while believing himself to be perfectly reasonable. He is the figure of unexamined confidence — certain of his own judgment while completely unaware of what he has become.

"Reverse" inverts that. The tool does not make you the donkey. It shows you where you might already be one.
